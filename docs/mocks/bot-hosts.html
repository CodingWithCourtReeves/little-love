Familiars are local-only by design. LittleLove will never connect your characters to OpenAI, Anthropic, OpenRouter, or any cloud LLM provider. Your bot host talks to Ollama (or a compatible local server) on your LAN, and that's the entire path.

Design notes — bot hosts & familiars

  • Tokenized palette (§11.4). Colors are CSS custom properties (--bg-canvas, --accent-user, --accent-partner, --accent-familiar, …) keyed off data-theme × data-theme-mode. Default is Hearth · light; the switcher in the top-right previews Linen, Vellum, and Twilight in either mode.
  • "Familiars," not "bots," in the headline. Reinforces the product's distinguishing concept. "Bot host" stays as the technical noun for the daemon (§9.2), because that's accurate — humans pair a host, then populate it with familiars. Push back here if you'd rather lean even further into the fantasy vocabulary.
  • Card == §9.1 CharacterCard, one-to-one. Each card surface shows name, persona excerpt (italic serif blockquote with a colored left rule), provider/model, behavior (Mention only / Smart / Always on per §9.6), context budget, tags, and current room membership. That covers every CharacterCard field except id.
  • Color-coded behavior & left-rules, now palette-driven. Smart = --accent-familiar, Mention only = --accent-partner, Always on = --accent-user. Swap to Vellum or Twilight and the three card variants re-paint cleanly without losing the affordance.
  • One privacy promise, stated once, in plain language. The familiar-tinted strip restates §9.4: "We will not ever ship a cloud-provider toggle." This is the product's main differentiator — it earns a dedicated, calm callout in the host management surface rather than being buried in a privacy doc.
  • Host card shows the connection's actual shape. OS, provider URL (in monospace), models, "LAN only" tag, pairing date. Court is the user; Court wants to see the truth of the connection. Hides nothing.
